The automation of hazardous, tedious, or resource-intensive tasks represents a primary advantage of robotics, enabling operations infeasible with human labor alone. The Aquapod is an amphibious robot for search-and-rescue and environmental monitoring, capable of land traversal, surface travel, submersion, and transitions between domains. However, achieving controllable mobility across these environments requires an all-terrain powertrain tailored to its operational requirements.

This proposal introduces the screwdrive propulsion system, a dual-modality drivetrain compatible with existing Aquapod platforms without modifications. In tumbling mode, it retains the robot's legacy locomotion for rough terrain. In aquatic mode, the screws function as propellers, enabling precise control over six degrees of freedom by modulating thrust vectoring. This enhances maneuverability, allowing directed propulsion on water/ice surfaces and underwater navigation.

Key innovations include screw geometry optimization for hybrid performance, integration with legacy hardware, and validation through field tests (diving wells, rivers, ice). Methods encompass dynamic modeling, PID tuning, and empirical benchmarking against prior Aquapod variants.

Scientific merit: Advances multi-domain robotics by bridging land-aquatic gaps, influencing amphibious design paradigms. Broader impacts: Low-cost platform democratizes environmental sensing, with applications in disaster response and ecology.
